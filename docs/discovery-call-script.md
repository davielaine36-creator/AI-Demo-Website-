# Laine Industries — Discovery Call Script

A simple, friendly guide for a 15–20 minute call. Goal: understand their
business, find the real problem, and recommend the smallest useful next step.
You are a helpful consultant, not a salesperson. Listen more than you talk.

---

## Opening script

> "Hey [Name], thanks for taking a few minutes. No pitch here — I mostly want to
> understand how your business runs day to day, and see if there's a simple way I
> can make it easier. If there is, great. If not, I'll tell you that too. Sound
> good?"

Then set the frame:

> "I'll ask a few quick questions about how leads and follow-up work for you
> right now, and then I'll tell you honestly what I'd do first — usually the
> smallest thing that solves the biggest headache."

---

## Questions to ask

**About the business**
- "Tell me what [Business] does in a sentence or two."
- "Who are your customers, and how do most of them find you?"

**About leads (the money questions)**
- "When a new customer reaches out — call, text, form — what happens next?"
- "Where does that info end up? Phone, notebook, spreadsheet, memory?"
- "How fast do you usually get back to them?"
- "Be honest — how often does a lead slip because follow-up didn't happen?"

**About the daily grind**
- "What feels the most repetitive or easy to forget right now?"
- "If one annoying thing disappeared tomorrow, what would it be?"

**About the website**
- "Do you have a website? What's working, what's not?"

**About success**
- "If we did this well, what would be different in 30–60 days?"

> Tip: after they answer the lead questions, reflect it back: "So it sounds like
> leads come in fine, but follow-up is where things slip when you're busy on a
> job — is that right?" This one move builds more trust than any pitch.

---

## How to explain the service (without sounding technical)

Keep it about outcomes, not tools. Swap jargon for plain words:

| Don't say | Say instead |
|---|---|
| "AI agent / LLM / automation pipeline" | "a system that drafts your follow-ups for you" |
| "CRM with pipeline stages" | "one simple place to see every lead and what's next" |
| "webhook / n8n integration" | "you get an alert the second a lead comes in" |
| "responsive front-end build" | "a clean website that works great on phones" |

Simple summary you can say out loud:

> "Basically, I put all your leads and customer info in one simple place, alert
> you when something needs attention, and have the system draft your follow-up
> messages so you're not starting from scratch. You stay in control — it just
> does the boring parts."

---

## How to recommend short intake vs. full intake

- **Short intake** — for people who are busy, early, or just curious. "Takes about
  five minutes and gives me enough to point you in the right direction."
- **Full intake** — for people who are clearly ready and want a tailored plan.
  "It's more detailed — about 10–15 minutes — but it lets me recommend something
  that actually fits instead of guessing."

Rule of thumb: **default to short intake.** Only push the full intake when they're
engaged and serious, or when the project is clearly bigger than one simple fix.

---

## How to close the call

Pick the smallest real next step and make it concrete:

> "Here's what I'd suggest: [one starter build], because it fixes the [their
> words] problem first without overbuilding. If that sounds right, the next step
> is a quick intake form so I can put together a simple plan and a price. Want me
> to send you the link?"

Then confirm the follow-up:

> "I'll follow up [day] with the plan. Does that work?"

Always leave with a scheduled next step or a sent link — never a vague "let me
think about it" with nothing attached.

---

## Handling tricky moments

**"I don't know what I need."**
> "That's completely normal — most people don't, and honestly it's my job to
> figure that out, not yours. Let's do this: I'll ask a couple more quick
> questions, and I'll tell you the one thing I'd fix first. If it's helpful, we
> keep going. If not, no harm done."

Then steer toward the Automation Audit or short intake.

**"How much does it cost?"**
> "Fair question. It depends on what we build, but starter builds usually land in
> the [low–mid range] — and I always recommend the smallest useful version first,
> so you're not paying for stuff you don't need. If you fill out the quick intake,
> I'll send you a clear number tied to exactly what you want. No surprises."

Never dodge the question — give a range, then tie the exact price to scope.

**"Can AI do everything?"**
> "Honestly, no — and I'd be careful with anyone who says it can. AI is great at
> drafting messages and organizing information fast, but you should always stay in
> control of decisions and what actually gets sent. I build it so AI does the
> boring, repetitive parts and you keep the judgment. That's the part that
> actually saves you time without creating a mess."
